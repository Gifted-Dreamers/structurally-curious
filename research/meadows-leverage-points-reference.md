# Donella Meadows: Leverage Points — Places to Intervene in a System (1999)

## Comprehensive Reference Document

---

## Origin

Meadows developed this list spontaneously at a meeting about NAFTA, GATT, and the WTO — the emerging global trade regime. She recalls thinking: "This is a HUGE NEW SYSTEM people are inventing! They haven't the SLIGHTEST IDEA how this complex structure will behave." She went to a flip chart and wrote the list on the spot, drawing on decades of systems analysis work influenced by Jay Forrester at MIT.

She explicitly states the list is "tentative and slithery" with "exceptions to every item" — it is not a recipe but a framework for thinking about where interventions actually have power.

---

## The 12 Leverage Points (Least to Most Effective)

### LP12: Constants, Parameters, Numbers (LEAST EFFECTIVE)

**What it is:** Adjusting numerical values within existing system structures — subsidies, tax rates, interest rates, wage levels, pollution standards, campaign contribution caps, air quality thresholds.

**Examples:** National debt/deficit levels, minimum wage, police spending, LA pollution standards, interest rate adjustments.

**Why it's dead last:** Parameters consume 99% of policy attention but rarely change system behavior. Interest rate adjustments haven't eliminated business cycles. Strict LA pollution standards didn't produce clean air. Increased police spending hasn't eliminated crime. Meadows: "Parameter changes rarely kick-start" stagnant systems.

**Key insight:** Most political battles are fought over parameters — fierce arguments about numbers within a structure nobody questions. This is where nearly everyone focuses, and it's where the least leverage exists.

---

### LP11: The Sizes of Buffers and Stabilizing Stocks

**What it is:** The capacity of stocks (reservoirs, inventories, savings) relative to their flows. Large buffers create stability; small buffers create vulnerability.

**Examples:**
- A huge bathtub with slow in/outflows (like a lake) vs. a rapid-flow river prone to catastrophic flooding
- Bank savings buffer you against income fluctuations
- Store inventories prevent stockouts
- Soil calcium content buffers against acid rain damage

**Why it's low-leverage:** Buffers are often physical entities — not easy to change, expensive to maintain. There's a trade-off: large buffers provide stability but reduce flexibility and increase costs.

**Ranking logic:** More powerful than parameters because buffer size determines how much shock a system can absorb. But still mostly physical — hard to adjust quickly.

---

### LP10: The Structure of Material Stocks and Flows

**What it is:** The physical infrastructure that determines how a system operates — road networks, power grids, population age distributions, building layouts.

**Examples:**
- Hungarian road system where ALL traffic from one side of the nation to the other passes through central Budapest — no parameter change (speed limits, traffic controls) fixes the structural pollution and congestion
- The baby boom swell moving through U.S. infrastructure: "first caused pressure on the elementary school system, then high schools, then colleges, then jobs and housing, and now we're looking forward to supporting its retirement"
- CFC molecules persisting in the ozone layer for decades
- Car fleet turnover taking 10-20 years
- Amory Lovins' building retrofits (demonstrating that better initial design is the real leverage)

**Why it's low-leverage:** Physical structures are "rarely a leverage point, because changing them is rarely quick or simple." The true leverage is in proper design in the first place — but once built, you're stuck with them.

---

### LP9: The Lengths of Delays, Relative to the Rate of System Change

**What it is:** The time lag between a system action and the feedback response. Delays relative to rates of change cause oscillations and overshoot.

**Examples:**
- The London hotel shower: "It wasn't even a bathtub, it was a shower — no buffering capacity. The water temperature took at least a minute to respond to my faucet twists. Guess what my shower was like. Right, oscillations from hot to cold and back to hot, punctuated with expletives."
- Multi-year power plant construction causing perpetual overcapacity/undercapacity cycles — "even the electricity industry in the world experiences long oscillations"
- Children maturing into reproductive age (biological delay)
- Technology adoption lags

**Critical principle:** "A delay in a feedback process is critical RELATIVE TO RATES OF CHANGE." Long delays combined with rapid growth cause overshoot and collapse.

**Key insight:** Often it's easier to slow the rate of change than to shorten delays. This connects to LP7 — slowing positive feedback loops.

---

### LP8: The Strength of Negative Feedback Loops, Relative to the Impacts They Correct

**What it is:** Self-correcting loops that maintain system stability around goals. A thermostat maintaining room temperature is the simplest example. Loop strength depends on monitoring accuracy, response speed, and directness of correction.

**Examples:**
- Preventive medicine strengthening immune system feedback
- Freedom of Information Act improving government accountability loops
- Pollution taxes capturing externalized costs previously hidden from markets
- Democracy — invented "to put self-correcting feedback between the people and their government," depending on "the free, full, unbiased flow of information back and forth between electorate and leaders." Weakens when "billions of dollars are spent to limit and bias and dominate that flow"
- Traditional fishing controls failing when technology (radar, drift nets) amplifies extraction power beyond what controls can correct

**Critical relationship:** Loop strength must match the impacts it corrects. As external impacts grow, negative loops must strengthen proportionally — or the system loses stability.

---

### LP7: The Gain Around Driving Positive Feedback Loops

**What it is:** Self-reinforcing loops that accelerate change — growth, erosion, collapse, epidemics.

**Examples:**
- Epidemics: more infected people infect others
- Compound interest: more money generates more interest
- Erosion: soil loss reduces vegetation, reducing root systems, accelerating erosion
- Population: "The more babies are born, the more people grow up to have babies"
- **"Success to the Successful"**: "Rich people collect interest; poor people pay it. Rich people pay accountants and lean on politicians to reduce their taxes; poor people can't. Rich people give their kids inheritances and good educations; poor kids lose out."
- Arms races
- Speculative financial bubbles

**Why slowing growth beats speeding correction:** "Slowing economic growth is a greater leverage point...than faster technological development." Chaos arises "when a system starts changing much, much faster than its negative loops can react to it" — fast-replicating bacteria, very infectious epidemics, wild speculative bubbles.

**Ranking logic:** More powerful than strengthening negative loops (LP8) because it's addressing the driver rather than the brake.

---

### LP6: The Structure of Information Flows (Who Does and Does Not Have Access to Information)

**What it is:** Creating or restoring missing feedback — connecting parts of the system that lack information about the consequences of their actions.

**THE TOXIC RELEASE INVENTORY (TRI) — Meadows' central example:**

In 1986, the U.S. government required that every factory releasing hazardous air pollutants report those emissions publicly every year. That's all — just report and publish.

"There was no law against those emissions, no fines, no determination of 'safe' levels, just information."

Result: Emissions dropped 40% by 1990. One chemical company reduced emissions by 90% simply to get off the "Top Ten Polluters list."

**Why it was so powerful:** It created "compelling feedback" where none existed. The public, communities, journalists, and investors could now see what was being released. No enforcement mechanism was needed — the information itself changed behavior by connecting consequences to actors.

**Why LP6 is a threshold:** This is where the list shifts from physical/structural interventions (LP12-7) to informational/design interventions (LP6-1). Adding or restoring information flows is more powerful than adjusting any of the parameters, buffers, structures, delays, or loop gains below it.

**Key insight:** Missing feedback is one of the most common causes of system malfunction. The system isn't broken — it's just blind to its own effects.

---

### LP5: The Rules of the System (Incentives, Punishments, Constraints)

**What it is:** Rules define "scope, boundaries, degrees of freedom." They include constitutions, laws, incentives, punishments, and informal agreements.

**Examples:**
- Imagining what would change if students graded teachers, or classes were graded collectively rather than individually
- The WTO: "a system with rules designed by corporations, run by corporations, for the benefit of corporations. Its rules exclude almost any feedback from any other sector of society"
- Constitutions as meta-rules (rules for writing rules)

**Who writes the rules matters more than the rules themselves:**

"Power over the rules is real power. That's why lobbyists congregate when Congress writes laws, and why the Supreme Court, which interprets and delineates the Constitution — the rules for writing the rules — has even more power than Congress."

**Ranking logic:** Rules shape all the dynamics below them. Change the rules and you change every parameter, every feedback loop, every information flow within the system.

---

### LP4: The Power to Add, Change, Evolve, or Self-Organize System Structure

**What it is:** A system's ability to change itself utterly by creating whole new structures, feedback loops, and rules. Biological evolution is the ultimate example — simple rules (DNA's four-letter code over billions of years) generating extraordinary diversity.

**Three forms of evolutionary raw material:**

1. **Biodiversity:** "Allowing species to go extinct is a systems crime, just as randomly eliminating all copies of particular science journals, or particular kinds of scientists, would be."

2. **Cultural diversity:** "Human cultures, of course, which are the store of behavioral repertoires, accumulated over not billions, but hundreds of thousands of years. They are a stock out of which social evolution can arise."

3. **Market/experimental diversity:** Freedom to experiment, try new approaches, fail and learn.

**The existential warning:** "Any system, biological, economic, or social, that gets so encrusted that it cannot self-evolve, a system that systematically scorns experimentation and wipes out the raw material of innovation, is doomed over the long term on this highly variable planet."

**Ranking logic:** Self-organization can change rules, goals, and structure. A system that can evolve can overcome any specific problem; a system that cannot evolve is trapped.

---

### LP3: The Goals of the System

**What it is:** The purpose or function that the entire system serves. More powerful than any individual feedback loop, rule, or structure — because the goal determines what all those subordinate elements optimize for.

**Critical distinction — stated goals vs. actual goals:**

Most corporations state their goal as profit or service, but their actual goal is "bringing the world (customers, suppliers, regulators) more and more under control."

**The Reagan example:**

Reagan demonstrated goal-leverage by repeatedly articulating "get government off our backs" instead of prior civic language about government serving citizens. This "thoroughly changed public discourse in the U.S. and even the world." He didn't change parameters or rules directly — he changed what the system was optimizing for.

**Who can access this leverage point:** Meadows notes that only those at the top of a system — leaders, founders, framers — can effectively shift system goals. This is why it's both powerful and rare.

**Ranking logic:** Change the goal and every rule, information flow, feedback loop, and parameter reorganizes to serve it. The goal is upstream of everything below it.

---

### LP2: The Mindset or Paradigm Out of Which the System Arises

**What it is:** The shared, usually unspoken assumptions about how reality works that give rise to system goals, rules, and structures. Meadows calls these "shared ideas in the minds of society."

**Examples of paradigms:**
- Money measures real value
- Growth is good
- Nature exists as a stock of resources to be converted to human purposes
- Ownership of land is legitimate
- Those with more deserve more
- The Egyptians built pyramids because they believed in an afterlife

**Examples of paradigm shifts:**
- Copernicus dethroning Earth from the center of the universe
- Einstein demonstrating matter-energy equivalence
- Adam Smith's invisible hand (self-interest serving collective good)
- Germ theory replacing miasma theory

Meadows quotes Emerson: paradigm shifts happen when an idea "clothes itself with societies, houses, cities."

**How paradigms change (via Thomas Kuhn):**
1. Point out anomalies and failures of the current paradigm relentlessly
2. Insert new-paradigm people into positions of public power and visibility
3. Don't waste time on reactionaries — work with the vast middle ground
4. Model the new paradigm visibly and persistently

**Why LP2 is so powerful:** Paradigms shape everything — goals, rules, structures, parameters. Change the paradigm and the entire system transforms. "Paradigm shifts totally transform systems."

**Why paradigms face fierce resistance:** The system IS the paradigm made physical. Changing the paradigm threatens every structure, institution, and power arrangement built on the old one.

---

### LP1: The Power to Transcend Paradigms (MOST EFFECTIVE)

**What it is:** The realization that "NO paradigm is 'true'" — that all understanding is "tremendously limited," and that every worldview is a tool, not a truth.

Meadows describes this as "keeping oneself unattached in the arena of paradigms" — what "the Buddhists call enlightenment."

**What this enables:** "If no paradigm is right, you can choose whatever one will help achieve your purpose." This creates "radical empowerment."

**The paradox:** People who operate at this level "bring down empires, get locked up or burned at the stake or crucified or shot." They are dangerous to every established system because they can see through any paradigm's claims to inevitability.

**Meadows' final reflection:** Mastery of systems "has less to do with pushing leverage points than it does with strategically, profoundly, madly letting go."

## Key Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. THE WRONG-DIRECTION PROBLEM

Jay Forrester's fundamental insight, which Meadows endorses fully:

"People know intuitively where leverage points are. Time after time I've done an analysis of a company, and I've figured out a leverage point...Then I've gone to the company and discovered that there's already a lot of attention to that point. Everyone is trying very hard to push it IN THE WRONG DIRECTION!"

Meadows restates: "Leverage points are not intuitive. Or if they are, we intuitively use them backward, systematically worsening whatever problems we are trying to solve."

**Example:** World leaders correctly identify growth as a leverage point (LP7) but push it upward when slowing it would actually solve the poverty and environmental problems they blame on insufficient growth.

**The higher the leverage, the worse the damage from wrong-direction pushing.** A parameter error (LP12) causes minor drift. A paradigm error (LP2) restructures civilization in the wrong direction.

### 2. WHY PEOPLE FOCUS ON THE WRONG (LOW) LEVERAGE POINTS

- 99% of attention goes to parameters (LP12), where there's the least leverage
- People fight fierce battles over numerical details within structures nobody questions
- Higher leverage points are more abstract, less visible, harder to quantify
- Higher leverage points face more institutional resistance — "the higher the leverage point, the more the system will resist changing it"
- The counterintuitive nature of systems means that when someone does identify a high-leverage point, "hardly anybody will believe us"

### 3. THE HIERARCHY: HOW HIGHER POINTS ENCOMPASS LOWER ONES

The list has an implicit nesting structure:
- **Paradigms** (LP2) generate **goals** (LP3)
- **Goals** determine **rules** (LP5)
- **Rules** shape **information flows** (LP6) and **feedback loop strength** (LP7-8)
- **Feedback loops** operate through **delays** (LP9), **structures** (LP10), **buffers** (LP11), and **parameters** (LP12)

Self-organization (LP4) is special — it's the capacity to generate entirely new structures at any level.

Transcending paradigms (LP1) is meta — it's the capacity to move between paradigm frameworks fluidly.

### 4. THE LP6 THRESHOLD

LP6 (information flows) marks a qualitative shift in the list. Below it (LP7-12), interventions are physical, structural, or parametric. Above it (LP5-1), interventions are informational, organizational, and conceptual. The TRI example demonstrates that simply making information visible — without any enforcement, punishment, or structural change — can produce 40% emission reductions. This is why Meadows places it as the gateway to the high-leverage zone.
