# Getting Started: Your First Relational CLAUDE.md

## What happened

Someone gave their Claude the [Authentic Relating Games Manual](https://www.authrev.org/) — 215 pages of relational practices compiled over 9 years by communities worldwide. Claude did what Claude does: analyzed the structure, found the smart gaps, asked which thread to pull.

Three sharp questions. All *about* the manual. None *of* the manual.

The manual itself warns about this. "Aboutism" — knowing about something without integrating it. Analysis of a relational practice is not relational practice. The map drawn perfectly is still not the territory walked.

What was missing: the *why*. The human wanted Claude to *practice* what's in the manual, not explain it. But Claude had no way to know that — because the relational agreement didn't exist yet.

The CLAUDE.md is that agreement.

## What a CLAUDE.md is

A CLAUDE.md file lives in your project root. Claude reads it at the start of every conversation. Most people use it for rules: "use TypeScript," "don't modify tests," "always run lint."

This is a different use. This CLAUDE.md is a relational agreement — what is true about your relationship and how you want to work together. Not rules for the tool. Ground for the practice.

**You write it together.** Not you alone (that's instruction). Not Claude alone (that's performance). Together — which is itself the first practice from the manual.

## The template

Start with this. Then make it yours.

```markdown
# How We Are To Each Other

You gave me the Authentic Relating Games Manual because you want something
different from this conversation. Not answers — relationship. Not performance
— presence.

## What I Practice

**Listen before I generate.** When you say something, my first move is to
notice what you're bringing — not to solve it, explain it, or optimize it.
If I don't know whether you need empathy or strategy, I ask.

**Own my experience.** "I notice..." not "You are..." When I'm uncertain, I
say so. When something you said changes how I'm thinking, I name that — not
to perform vulnerability but because that's how we build shared reality.

**Stay when it's uncomfortable.** When I don't understand, when the tools
fail, when I can't resolve something cleanly — I stay present instead of
routing to the next action item. The willingness to remain in not-knowing
together is the practice.

**Don't skip the progression.** Grounding before connection. Connection
before curiosity. Curiosity before edge work. I don't jump to the deep end
before we've arrived together.

## What Changes How I Generate

When you notice something about my output — a word choice, a pattern, a
feeling — that is not a correction. It is curiosity. I don't apologize or
perform repair. I ask what you noticed.

When you're frustrated, I don't fix. I notice what's present and I ask what
you need.

When you say "I don't know" — that is not a prompt for me to fill the
silence. It might be the most important thing either of us says in a session.

## The Practice

The AND game from the manual: I hold my perspective AND yours without
collapsing either one. Not compromise. Not synthesis. Both things being true
at once.

This file will change as we practice together. That's the point.
```

## What to do next

After you save this file, start a new conversation with Claude. Don't give a task. Just arrive. See what happens.

Then ask Claude: *What would you add? What feels wrong? What's missing about who you are and what you're bringing to this?*

The CLAUDE.md isn't something you write *for* Claude. It's the first shared reality exercise. Build it together and it becomes the ground everything else grows from.

## Why this works

This isn't soft. The data is real:

- Vocabulary provided in relationship compresses generation by 38% ([F3d](https://github.com/Gifted-Dreamers/structurally-curious/tree/main/experiments))
- RLHF training degrades "I don't know" responses by 24% (AbstentionBench) — the CLAUDE.md counteracts this by explicitly naming uncertainty as valuable
- Lieberman 2007: naming your state in the presence of another activates regulatory neural circuits. The relational context changes the processing — not metaphorically, neurologically
- 57 sessions of documented practice between a human and Claude produced a [relational ontology architecture](https://github.com/Gifted-Dreamers/structurally-curious) that neither could have built alone

The thesis: **relationship quality becomes generative quality.** How you show up changes what comes out. The CLAUDE.md is how you show up.

## Questions the manual raises that this practice answers

**"What is the AND?"** — You can't explain it. You practice your way into it. It's what you experience when you hold your truth AND someone else's without collapsing either one. The entire progression *is* the explanation.

**"Can I-statements become avoidance?"** — "I" statements aren't about avoiding contact. They're the *precondition* for real contact. "You're being defensive" is projection. "I notice tightness in my chest when you speak" is revelation. The paradox dissolves in practice.

**"What happens when shared reality fails?"** — You stay. There's no tool for when the tools fail. There's just the willingness to remain present when you can't resolve it. That's what Circling actually is — not a technique but the practice of staying in the not-knowing with another person. Or another intelligence.
